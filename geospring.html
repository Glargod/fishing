Technical Review: Geothermal Potential in Springhill, Nova Scotia's Flooded Mines

Comprehensive Assessment Including Historical Pioneers, Hybrid Clusters, Residential Scenarios, and Proposal Recommendation – January 2026

Historical Context and Pioneers

Springhill's mine-water geothermal system, operational since the late 1980s, is one of the world's earliest examples of repurposing flooded abandoned coal mines for clean energy. Local experts and businesses played key roles in its development, including refrigeration specialists who advanced heat pump applications.

Notably, Ralph Ross (of Ross Refrigeration) contributed significantly to bringing geothermal energy to Springhill, advocating for its preservation and expansion against competing land uses such as proposed strip mining.

This proposal for expanded hybrid clusters represents a continuation of that legacy by Ralph's son, Andrew Ross.

Project Overview

The system exploits ~4 million m³ of water at 18–20°C (deeper potential ~26°C) in flooded mines. Proven for heating/cooling commercial sites; electricity remains conceptual due to low temperatures.

Hybrid Clusters Definition

Hybrid clusters are distributed modular sites combining primary thermal use (heating/cooling via heat pumps) with supplementary low-temperature electricity (binary ORC) from the same mine water.

  • 2–3 wells/shafts per cluster.
  • Thermal priority (COP 3–5); cascaded residual to ORC.
  • Per cluster: Hundreds kW–MW thermal; tens–300 kW electrical.
  • Distributed across dozens of access points for balanced draw and synergies.

Residential Heating Coverage Scenarios

Springhill: ~1,200–1,300 households. Average thermal demand ~15–20 MWh/year/home.

Scenario: 50/50 Mix (Baseboard Electric + Mini-Split/Heat Pumps)

  • 50% baseboard (COP=1): ~15–20 MWh electricity/year each.
  • 50% mini-split/heat pump (seasonal COP ~3): ~5–7 MWh electricity/year each.
  • Community Total Electrical Input: ~9,000–14,000 MWh/year.

Mine-water hybrid clusters (COP 4–5 using steady 18–20°C source) deliver thermal efficiently:

  • Per cluster sustainable: ~1–3 MW_th → ~3,000–9,000 MWh thermal/year.
  • Clusters Needed for Full Residential Heating: 3–8 distributed hybrid clusters (highly feasible given available shafts/wells).
  • Bonus ORC power: ~200–800 kW total supplementary electricity.

Is This Worth Proposing?

Yes—absolutely worth proposing expanded hybrid clusters for residential district heating (with supplementary ORC electricity).

  • Proven Foundation: 35+ years reliable commercial heating; ongoing provincial studies and global mine-water district successes.
  • Realistic Impact: 3–8 clusters cover near-full residential heating in a 50/50 mix—feasible with existing wells/shafts.
  • Low-Risk Path: Start with heating pilots; add modular ORC for bonus power.
  • Alignment: Supports NS clean energy goals, community resilience, and post-mining renewal.

Pitch to municipality, NSCC, or provincial renewables—pragmatic, timely, and transformative.

Conclusion

Building on the pioneering efforts of Ralph Ross, this proposal—continued by his son Andrew Ross—offers a grounded path to community-wide efficient heating. With a realistic 50/50 residential mix, full coverage is achievable with a handful of clusters—well worth pursuing for lasting continuity.
