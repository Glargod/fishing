Technical Review: Geothermal Potential in Springhill, Nova Scotia's Flooded Mines

Updated Assessment Including Distributed Multi-Cluster Potential – January 2026

Project Overview

Springhill, Nova Scotia, pioneered mine-water geothermal utilization since the late 1980s, exploiting flooded abandoned coal mines with ~4 million cubic meters of water at proven 18–20°C (deeper potential up to 26°C). The interconnected underground network supports direct heating/cooling via heat pumps for industrial, commercial, and community sites.

Current focus remains thermal applications; electricity generation unexplored commercially due to low temperatures.

Current System and Resource

  • Access Points: ~25 geothermal wells drilled since 1990s; 17 active (6 municipal, 11 private). Multiple shafts and boreholes distributed across town and industrial park enable potential multi-site development.
  • Technology: Open-loop heat pumps; proven reliable for heating/cooling.
  • Scale: Dozens of connections; vast reservoir supports expansion.

Potential for Multiple Distributed Clusters

The interconnected flooded workings and numerous access points (wells/shafts) allow distributed development: multiple independent or linked sites hosting modular ORC units or hybrid heat-power clusters. This "whole greater than parts" approach leverages:

  • Balanced resource draw to minimize local cooling/recharge issues.
  • Modular scaling with small ORC units (50–300 kW each).
  • Proximity to loads for efficient district heating + power co-production.
  • Synergies: cascaded heat use, shared monitoring, potential grid interconnection.

Realistic total potential remains constrained by ultra-low temperatures; distributed clusters enhance feasibility for heating dominance with supplementary power.

Electricity Generation Feasibility (ORC)

Hybrid cascading (heating first, residual to binary ORC) considered, with modular units per cluster.

Assessment: At 18–20°C, net efficiency ~4–7%. Per modular unit: tens to ~200–300 kW optimistic. Distributed across sites: sub-1 MW total likely viable; higher requires warmer deeper access.

No global precedents for power below ~30–40°C; focus worldwide on heating.

Challenges and Risks

  • Thermodynamic limits at low ΔT.
  • Sustainable draw across distributed sites critical.
  • Fouling/corrosion management.
  • Economics favor heating over power.

Conclusion and Recommendations

Springhill's proven heating success and multiple access points offer strong foundation for distributed clusters—amplifying benefits through modular, balanced deployment. Electricity remains marginal but could supplement in hybrids.

Recommendations: Prioritize deeper wells for warmer water; pilot modular hybrid at one cluster; expand district heating networks.
