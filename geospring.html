Technical Review: Geothermal Potential in Springhill, Nova Scotia's Flooded Mines

Updated Assessment Including Distributed Multi-Cluster Potential and Proposed ORC Enhancements – January 2026

Project Overview

Springhill pioneered mine-water geothermal since the late 1980s, using flooded coal mines (~4 million m³ water at 18–20°C, deeper potential ~26°C). Proven for direct heating/cooling via heat pumps; electricity unexplored due to low temperatures.

Current System and Resource

  • Access Points: ~25 wells drilled; 17 active. Multiple shafts enable distributed development.
  • Technology: Open-loop heat pumps; reliable thermal use.
  • Scale: Dozens of connections; vast interconnected reservoir.

Potential for Multiple Distributed Clusters

Numerous shafts/wells allow modular clusters (2–3 turbines/site). Benefits: balanced draw, proximity to loads, synergies in monitoring/heating networks. Realistic total: sub-1 MW electricity (heating dominant).

Proposed ORC Enhancements

Insulated Buffer Tanks with Three Out-of-Sync (Phased) Evaporators per Turbine

Insulated tanks store mine water, feeding three parallel evaporators in staggered phases for smoother vapor flow to one turbine.

  • Benefits: Stabilizes flow/temperature; enhances transfer; mitigates fouling; reduces turbine stress.
  • Feasibility: Common buffering in heat pumps; phased parallel rare in steady geothermal but seen in variable sources. Marginal gains at low ΔT.
  • Challenges: Added cost/complexity; no direct mine-water ORC precedents.

Using Cooled Mine Water in Condenser Pools/Basins Before Reinjection

Post-evaporator cooled water (~10–12°C) routes to basins for condenser cooling, then reinjects.

  • Benefits: Minor recovery; potential evaporative boost in dry conditions.
  • Feasibility: Uncommon; standard binary ORC uses air-cooling or separate loops to avoid fouling.
  • Challenges: High scaling/corrosion risk; insufficient ΔT; water loss in open systems. Air-cooling preferred.

Overall Electricity Feasibility (ORC)

Enhancements improve reliability but do not overcome low-temperature limits (~4–7% net efficiency). Per turbine: tens–300 kW; distributed total: sub-1 MW likely.

Challenges and Risks

  • Thermodynamic constraints at ultra-low ΔT.
  • Sustainable multi-site draw.
  • Fouling, complexity in enhancements.

Conclusion and Recommendations

Proposed enhancements (phased evaporators with buffers, condenser basins) offer innovative stability but marginal impact at proven temperatures. Distributed clusters amplify heating value; power remains supplementary.

Recommendations: Model/pilot enhancements; explore deeper warmer water; prioritize heating expansion.
