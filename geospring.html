How the System Works

Open-Loop Baseline (Ralph Ross Original Invention)

Mine water (~18°C) flows through the Y-strainer and variable-speed pump into the plate heat exchanger (HX1). Heat transfers to R-32 refrigerant, driving the direct-DC inverter compressor. Chilled return water drops 100 ft, spinning the micro-hydro turbine for on-site DC power. Refrigerant serves the four zoned 4-way ceiling cassettes.

Closed-Loop Configuration (Andrew Ross Piggyback Enhancement)

A sealed glycol loop circulates through submerged coils in the mine, exchanging heat at HX1 without mine water entering the home. This provides durability and easier permitting while keeping the rest of the system unchanged.

Solar Integration (Collaborative Enhancement with Rob Charest)

Solar PV connects directly to the DC bus, supplementing micro-hydro for greater self-sufficiency.

Recommended Airflow: ~400 CFM per ton (350–450 CFM/ton range) for optimal comfort with the ceiling cassettes.

Overall Mine Water Geothermal Concept

Feasibility in Amherst / Springhill

Springhill pioneered mine-water geothermal in the 1980s with successful installations in public and industrial buildings. The flooded mines provide a vast, stable thermal reservoir at ~18–20°C. The design scales this proven resource to homes with closed-loop durability and solar resilience. It is technically strong for Cumberland County and aligns with regional assessments of residential potential.
